HW 7: Holoportation & Collaborative Work in VR

Before even looking at the rest of the week’s notes, the first thing that caught my eye about Microsoft’s Holoportation is the fact that it could be used to “transport” humans to places where they can’t be but are needed. My initial thought went immediately to occupations. Doctors that could check on patients remotely, electricians that could troubleshoot generators remotely, plumbers that could tell you how to fix your own sink leak. The options are only limited by the occupations and services people provide.

This tool takes communication to the next level. Before we had the technology to only see and speak with people who are physically accessible. Then we had the technology to see them and speak with them even when they were halfway across the globe. Now, we have the technology to see what people see and hear, to be with them ‘physically’.

In essence, this creates the ability to ‘be’ with people more easy. In a lot of ways this makes our lives easier. Everything from job interviews to examinations will change dramatically. The technology behind Holoportation is simple too, and it can be incorporated into our mobile phones, at least from what the article suggests.

However, one thing comes to mind when discussing such a tool.

“It is very common,” Rosenstein says, “for humans to develop things with the best of intentions and for them to have unintended, negative consequences.”

This was quoted by a Silicon Valley developer discussing the negative aspects of mobile phones. Mobile phones have drastically increased the way we communicate, and there have been negative aspects to that. For example, people are more connected but more lonely. Will the Holoportation affect humans similarly? Will it cause unforeseen changes in the way we interact and behave? Should that impact its development?
